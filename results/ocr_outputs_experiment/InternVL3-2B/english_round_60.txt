Hydrogen (¹₁H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹₁H and²H, and³H.¹H and²H are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10–21 s).[¹¹]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²H is deuterium and³H is tritium.[¹²] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols²H and³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[¹⁴]¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[¹⁬�] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioactive isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.[¹⁰]
