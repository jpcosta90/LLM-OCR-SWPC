Hydrogen (¹₏H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²H, and³H. ¹H and ²H are stable, while³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10⁻²¹ s).[4][4][nb 1] Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²²H is deuterium and³H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deutrium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²²H and³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[5][6]¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioactive isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
