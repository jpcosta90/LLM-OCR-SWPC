Hydrogen (¹₁H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²²H, and ³⁶H.¹H and ²H¹ are stable, while ³I₃ and ²H¹ have a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zentoseconds (10⁻²¹ s). Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²H¹ is deuterium and ³H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²H and ³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. In early radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
