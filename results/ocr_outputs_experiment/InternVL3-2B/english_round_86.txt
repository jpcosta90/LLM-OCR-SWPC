Hydrogen (¹ₐH) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²₃H, and ³³H. ¹₃H and ²³H are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavy isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10⁻¹⁰ s).[⁴][⁵]¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate[¹⁶] In addition, the symbols D and T are sometimes used for deutrium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²⁶H and ³³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[⁶][⁸]¹H1, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate[¹⁰] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today[¹₁].
