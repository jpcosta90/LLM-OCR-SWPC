Hydrogen (^1H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ^1H, ^2H, and ^3H. ^1H and ^2H are stable, while ^3H has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][1nb1] Heavy isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10^–21 s). Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that still remain in common use today: ^2H is deuterium and ^3H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ^2H and ^3H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas. [6][9][10] Heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.

During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioactive radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
