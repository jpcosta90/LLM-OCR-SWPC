Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹² ²H, and ¹³ ³H. ¹H and ²H are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1zeptosecond (10−21 s). Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²²H is deuterium and ³³H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²²H and ³³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate it. During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
