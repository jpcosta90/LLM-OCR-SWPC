Hydrogen (¹ₐ H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹ᵢ H, and ³H. ¹ᵢ H and ²H⁸ are stable, while ³H²H have a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavy isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10⁻²¹ s). Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²H⁸ is deuterium and ³H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $_2$H and $_3$H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. Some other heavy radiatio isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
