Hydrogen (1H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ^1H, and ^2H. ^1H and ^2H are stable, while ^3H has a half-life of 12.32 years.[4][nb]

Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zetacept (10^{-21} s).[5][6]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ^1H is designated as deuteron [7] and ^3H is tritium [8]. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deutrium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²H and ³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.⁹ symbols and no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate [10].
