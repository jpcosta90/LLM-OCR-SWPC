Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Isotopes of hydrogen have three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^\text{2}^\text{H}\) and \(^\text{3}^\text{H}\). \(^\text{2}^\text{H}\) and \(^\text{3}^\text{H}\) are stable, while \(^\text{3}^\text{H}\) has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nbl 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zetacept (10\(^{-21}\) s).\.[5][6]

Hydrogen (1H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^\text{H}$, \(^\text{2H}\), and \(^\text{3H}\). \(^\text{H}\) and \(^\text{2H}\) are stable, while \(^\text{3H}\) has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nbl 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all 
are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zetacept (10\(^{-21}\) s). [5][6]
