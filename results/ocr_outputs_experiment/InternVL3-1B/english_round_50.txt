Isotopes of hydrogen

**Article**

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (₁H), has three naturally occurring isotopes: **₁H**, **²H**, and **³H}.** ¹H and **²H** are stable, while **³H** has a **half-life of 12.32 years].** Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zepto SEC (10⁻²¹ s).** [5][6] **Hydrogen** is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: **²H** is deuterium [7], and **³H** is tritium.** The symbols **D** and **T** are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; **IUPAC** (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols **²H** and **³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.**[9] symbols **¹H,** with no neutrons, may be called **protium to disambiguate.**[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
