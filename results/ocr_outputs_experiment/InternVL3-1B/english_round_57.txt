Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen \( ^1H \) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \( ^2H \) and \( ^3H \). \( ^1H \) and \( ^2H \) are stable, while \( ^3H \) has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nb1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zettasentence 100s [5][6]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \( ^2H \) is deuterium and \( ^3H \) is tritium. [7] The symbols \( D \) and \( T \) are sometimes used for deutium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \( ^{2}^{1}H \) and \( ^{3}^{1}H \), to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas. [9] symbols \( ^{2}^{1}H \) and \( ^{3}^{1}H \), with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. [10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
