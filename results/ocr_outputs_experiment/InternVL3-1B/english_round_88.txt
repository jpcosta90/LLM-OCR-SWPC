Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

**Hydrogen (₁H,²H, and ³H)** has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^{1H}\), \(^{2H}\), and \(^{3H}\). \(^{1H}\) and \(^{2H}\) are stable, while \(^{3H}\) has a half-life of 12.32 years. [#4][nb1] Hydroisites also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeta particle (10⁻² s).]

Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeta particle (10⁻²1 s).

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \(^{2H}\) is deuteron\[7\] and \(^{3H}\) is tritium. The symbols \(D\) and \(T\) are sometimes used for deuternium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(^{2H}\) and \(^{3H}\), to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas. [10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
