Isotopes of hydrogen

Article Talk 
From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia


Hydrogen (H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹ H, ² H and ³ H; 1 H is by far most stable,[5] while 3 H has a half-life of 12.32 years.[6][7].[8]. Heavier isotopes also exist, all are synthetic[9], have very short-lived radiocytometry (half-lives less than) second [0–-s).[a]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today. 2 H is deuterium,[1] and³His tritrium.[6]. The symbols D[9][i], T[a, b],[c].[d], N [e,f,g,h,i,j,k,l,m,n,o,p,q,r,s,t,u,v,w,x,y,z,a,b,c,d,e,f,g,h].

The IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols; but recommends the standard isotopic symbol for 1 H,²H(2¹₁),³ to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting chemical formulas.[6].[7]. During early study radioactivity some other heavy radiotopes were given names such as [m,n,o,p,q,r,s,t,u,v,w,x,y,z] named protron and triton[8]; however, these terms are no longer used today.
