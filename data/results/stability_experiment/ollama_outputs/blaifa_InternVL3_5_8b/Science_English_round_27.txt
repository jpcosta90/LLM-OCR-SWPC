[ edit ]     The word "science" is derived directly into many languages and it has been argued that there are three branches. Modern definitions 
of scientific inquiry include both systematic hypothesis testing [1] [23–24] or predictions about the universe.[5][69–71]

Science (from Latin sciēns, meaning 'knowledge') broadly refers to any systematized body of knowledge,[a ] whether in the form of a natural 
philosophy , metaphysics and theology. While science is usually understood as involving only scientific method[3] or empirical evidence that can be tested by observation, its relationship with mathematics has been described through history 

science . The word "scientia" comes from Latin for knowledge (cf.[74–91]). Aristotle defined the term in two 
ways: either 'knowledge gained about things' [68][2] or as a form of practical wisdom. Philosophers have taken several positions on which area has more weight, and whether philosophy itself should be considered 

the science . Science is typically regarded as separate from art[31–50], literature,[74],[90]; religion , history ; metaphysics 
and theology.[2][68] While referred to as the formal sciences, it includes logic, mathematics, statistics [ 1 ] and computer science. In contrast with other disciplines that rely on inductive reasoning instead of scientific method as their main 

methodology,[74–90], applied engineering are considered a separate branch[32][68] which combines elements from both pure sciences.[5]

The history of science spans the majority of the historical record, with the earliest identifiable predecessors to modern science dating to 
the Bronze Age in Egypt and Mesopotamia (c. 3000–1200 BCE). Their contributions to mathematics , astronomy , medicine[69] etc., including early forms or prototypes of what would become Western philosophy . These developments were made during the Vedic period 

of ancient India, which lasted until around c.5th century CE and is considered by some scholars as one possible precursor 
to science.[74–80][132]
