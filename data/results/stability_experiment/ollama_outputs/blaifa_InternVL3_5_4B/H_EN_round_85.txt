Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia


Hydrogen (H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: 1 H , 2 H and 3 H . The symbols for deuterium and tritium are sometimes used interchangeably with these numbers; since both have no neutrons themselves but contain one neutron respectively. They also differ in their chemical properties, although due to the small mass difference they behave very similarly as other hydrogen isotopes[10] [8]. All stable elements except for protium (which has three naturally occurring forms) are radioactive.[9]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain commonly used today: 4 H is deuterium and 3 H is tritum. The symbols D and T sometimes refer to hydrogen atom number of mass; these numbers can be written in many ways depending on notation style or system.[10] (e.g., the International Union Pure Chemistry) accepts said symbol, but recommends standard isotopic systems for both atomic masses[8]. In terms with 4 H , a common alternative is called deuterium and tritum to avoid confusion. It was discovered by studying radioactivity; some other heavy radioactive were given names due in early study of radioactivity.
