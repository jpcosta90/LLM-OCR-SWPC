Scientific Method  

The history of science spans the majority of the historical record, with the earliest identifiable predecessors to modern science dating to The Bronze Age in Egypt and Mesoamerica (~3000–1200 BCE).[4] Their contributions to mathematics, astronomy, medicine, architecture[5][6]
and philosophy are often considered a precursor to Western classical learning.[7]

The scientific method is the typical paradigm by which science operates. Its development during
late medieval scholarship , whereby formal attempts were made to provide explanations of events in The physical world based on natural causes; while further advances 
during 14–20th century Europe and later periods led from Hindu-Buddhist systems, through developments into Western scientific rationalism[8][9] 

The Scientific Method  

Science is a systematic discipline that builds and organises knowledge in the form of testable hypotheses [3a],[d,e,[g]] predictions about the universe.[4]

Modern science comprises five major branches: The natural sciences , which study the physical world, 
and the social sciences[10] ,  or (often) "soft" sciences that deal with human behaviour and its underlying causes. While referred to as formal [f],[h]
the term is often loosely used for all types of science.[i][j]

The natural sciences are typically regarded as separate because they rely on deductive reasoning instead
of the scientific method[4]as their main methodology,[k,l,m] while applied engineering and medicine (see also 
applied physics) [n,o,p],[q]] use both.

The history of science spans The majority 17th century to modern times, with most major advances in knowledge made during this time period.[r]
