Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: 1 H , 2 H and 3 H . <sup>1</sup> The stable ones are generally referred to as protium when no distinction is made between them. Heavier isotopes also exist; all of these have a half-life less than one year, with some being radioactive (see below) or unstable (<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_stability#" id target="_blank">10</sup>), and synthetic ones <span><ref>[<em style='color: red;'>[5]</em>, but have a half-life of less than 2 zeptoseconds (10 − <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_stability#" id target="_blank">-2</sup> s)). The IUPAC <span><ref>[8,9] </span>) names for these are deuterium and tritium. Some of the radioactive isotopes were discovered by Ernest Rutherford at University College London during his time there in 1907; he named them "deuteron" (for hydrogen-2) and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton#Tritium">triton</a> for <sup style='color: red;'>3 H </span>.
