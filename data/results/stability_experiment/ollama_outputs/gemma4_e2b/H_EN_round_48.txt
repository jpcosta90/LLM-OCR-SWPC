Isotopes of hydrogen
Article Talk
Isotopes of hydrogen $\text{}^1\text{H}$, $\text{}^2\text{H}$, and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ are stable, while $\text{}^3\text{H}$ has a half-life of 12.32 years. $\text{}^1\text{H}$ and $\text{}^2\text{H}$ are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium. From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia Hydrogen $\text{}^1\text{H}$ has three naturally occurring isotopes: $\text{}^1\text{H}$, $\text{}^2\text{H}$, and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC ($\text{International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry}$) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{}^2\text{H}$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas.[9] Heavier isotopes also exist; all $\text{}^1\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
