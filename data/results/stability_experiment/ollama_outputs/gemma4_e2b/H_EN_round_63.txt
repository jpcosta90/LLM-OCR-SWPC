Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{}^2\text{H}$ is deuterium[7] and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ is tritium.[8] The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC ($\text{International Union of Pure}$
Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{}^2\text{H}$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical}$
formulas.[9] $\text{}^1\text{H}$, with no $\text{neutrons}$, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early
study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such
names are rarely used today.
