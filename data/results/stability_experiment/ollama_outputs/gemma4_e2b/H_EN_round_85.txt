Isotopes of hydrogen
Article Talk isotopes of hydrogen
From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
Hydrogen ($\text{}^1\text{H}$) has three naturally occurring isotopes: $\text{}^1\text{H}$, $\text{}^2\text{H}$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$. $\text{}^1\text{H}$ and $\text{}^2\text{H}$ are stable, while $\text{}^3\text{H}$ has a half-life of $12.32$ years.[5] Isotopes of hydrogen also exist: all are synthetic and have a half-life of $12.32$ years.[6] $\text{}^1\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] $\text{}^2\text{H}$ is deuterium[7] and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ is tritium[8]. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{}^2\text{H}$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas.[9]
