Isotopes of hydrogen
Article $\text{1H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.
Heavier isotopes also exist; all
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in common use today: $\text{2H}$ is deuterium[7] and $\text{3H}$ is tritium[8]. The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC ($\text{International}$ $\text{Union}$ of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{2H}$ and $\text{3H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas.[9]
