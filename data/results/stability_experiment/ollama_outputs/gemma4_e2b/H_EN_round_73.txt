Isotopes of hydrogen
$\text{}^1\text{H}$, $\text{}^2\text{H}$, and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ are stable, while $\text{}^3\text{H}$ has half-life of 12.32 years.[4]nb 1
Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than $10^{21}$ s.[5][6]
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in common use today:
Deuterium ($\text{}^2\text{H}$), and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ is tritium.[8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC chemical formulas. $\text{}^1\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
