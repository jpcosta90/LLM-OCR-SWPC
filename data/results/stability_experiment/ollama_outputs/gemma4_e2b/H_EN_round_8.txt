Isotopes of hydrogen
$\text{}^1\text{H}$, $\text{}^2\text{H}$, and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ are
stable, while $\text{}^3\text{H}$ has a half-life of 12.32 years. $[\text{4}]\text{nb}$
Heavier isotopes also exist; all
are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 10.21 s $[\text{5}]\text{6}$
Hydrogen is the only element
whose isotopes have different names in
common use today.
$\text{}^2\text{H}$ is deuterium $[\text{7}]$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ is tritium $[\text{8}]$ The symbols D and T are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC
(International Union of Pure and
Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{}^1\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. $[\text{10}]$
During the early
study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such
names are rarely used today.
