Isotopes of hydrogen
Article $\text{1H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. [10]
Heavier isotopes also exist; all
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{2H}$ is deuterium [7] and $\text{3H}$ is tritium. [8] The symbols D and T are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and
Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{2H}$ and $\text{3H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas.[9]
