From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
Hydrogen ($\text{}^{1}\text{H}$) has three naturally occurring $\text{}^{1}\text{H}$, and $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; $\text{}^{1}\text{H}$ and $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ are
stable, while $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$ has a half-life of $12.32$ years. [4]nb [5]
Isotopes of hydrogen have different names in
common use today: $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ is $\text{deuterium}$ [7] and $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$ is $\text{tritium}$ [8]. The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC
(International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ and $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas. [9]
