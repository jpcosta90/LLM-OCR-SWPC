Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ is deuterium[7] and $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$ is tritium[8]. The symbols D and T are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC ($\text{International}$ $\text{Union}$ of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ and $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$ [5].
Hydrogen has a half-life of $12.32$ years. $\text{}^{1}\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.
Heavier isotopes also exist; all $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ are stable, while $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$ has a half-life of $12.32$ years. $\text{}^{10}\text{H}$ is $\text{zeptosecond}$ [5][6].
