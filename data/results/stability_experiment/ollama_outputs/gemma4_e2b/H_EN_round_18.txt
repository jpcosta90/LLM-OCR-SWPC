Isotopes of hydrogen
Article $\text{}^1\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. $[10]$
Heavier isotopes also exist; all
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{}^2\text{H}$ is deuterium $[7]$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ is tritium, $[8]$ The symbols D and T are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure
and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{}^2\text{H}$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical}$
formulas.[9]
