Heavier isotopes also exist; all
are synthetic and have a half-life of $10^{-21}$ s.[5][6]
Hydrogen is the only $\text{element}$ whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{2H}$ is $\text{deuterium}$[7] and $\text{3H}$ is $\text{tritium}$[8]. The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC ($\text{International Union of Pure}$
and $\text{Applied Chemistry}$) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{2H}$ and $\text{3H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical}$ formulas.[9]
$\text{1H}$, with no $\text{neutrons}$, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early
study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given $\text{names}$, but such
names are rarely used today.
