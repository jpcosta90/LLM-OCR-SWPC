From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
Hydrogen ($\text{1H}$) has three naturally occurring isotopes: $\text{1H}$, $\text{2H}$, and $\text{3H}$ sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; $\text{IUPAC}$ ($\text{International}$ $\text{Union}$ of Pure and Applied $\text{Chemistry}$) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{2H}$ and $\text{3H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical}$ $\text{formulas}$.$[9]$
$\text{1H}$, with no $\text{neutrons}$, may be called protium to disambiguate. $[10]$ During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
