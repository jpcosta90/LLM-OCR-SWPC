Isotopes of hydrogen
Article $\text{1H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. [10]
Heavier isotopes also exist; all
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{2H}$ is deuterium [7] and $\text{3H}$ is tritium, [8] The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC ($\text{International}$ $\text{Union}$ of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{2H}$ and $\text{3H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical}$ formulas. [9]
$\text{1H}$, with no $\text{neutrons}$, may be called protium to disambiguate. [10]
During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such
names are rarely used today.
