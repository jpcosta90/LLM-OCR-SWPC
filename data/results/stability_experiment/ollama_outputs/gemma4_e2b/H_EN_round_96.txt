Isotopes of hydrogen
$\text{H}$ has three naturally occurring isotopes: $\text{}^1\text{H}$, $\text{}^2\text{H}$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$. $\text{}^1\text{H}$ and $\text{}^2\text{H}$ are stable, while $\text{}^3\text{H}$ has half-life of $12.32$ years.[4]
Heavier isotopes also exist; all
$\text{Hydrogen}$ is the only $\text{element}$ whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{}^2\text{H}$ is $\text{deuterium}$[7] and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ is $\text{tritium}$[8]. The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC ($\text{International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry}$) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{}^2\text{H}$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical formulas}$.[9]
During the early
study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given $\text{names}$, but such
names are rarely used today.
