Isotopes of hydrogen
Article $\text{1H}$, with
isotopes $\text{2H}$, and $\text{3H}$. $\text{1H}$ and $\text{2H}$ are
stable, while $\text{3H}$ has a half-life of $12.32$ years. $[\text{4}]\text{nb}$ $\text{10}$
Heavier isotopes also exist; all
are synthetic and have a half-life of less than $10^{-21}$ seconds $[\text{5}][6]$
Hydrogen is the only element
whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{2H}$ is deuterium$[\text{7}]$ and $\text{3H}$ is tritium $[\text{8}]$ The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC
(International Union of Pure and
Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{2H}$ and $\text{3H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical}$ formulas.$[\text{9}]$
$\text{1H}$, with no $\text{neutrons}$, may be called protium to disambiguate. $[\text{10}]$ During the early
study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such
names are rarely used today.
