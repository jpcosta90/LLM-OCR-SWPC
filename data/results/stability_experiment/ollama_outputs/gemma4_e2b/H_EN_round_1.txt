Isotopes of hydrogen
Article Talk
Isotopes of hydrogen $\text{1H}$, $\text{2H}$, and $\text{3H}$ are stable, while $\text{3H}$ has a half-life of $12.32$ years. $[\text{4}]\text{nb}$ $\text{1}$
Heavier isotopes also exist; all
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{2H}$ is deuterium$[\text{7}]$ and $\text{3H}$ is tritium$[\text{8}]$. The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; $\text{IUPAC}$ ($\text{International Union of Pure}$
Applied Chemistry}$) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{2H}$ and $\text{3H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical}$
formulas. $[\text{9}]$
$\text{1H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. $[\text{10}]$ During the early
study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such
names are rarely used today.
