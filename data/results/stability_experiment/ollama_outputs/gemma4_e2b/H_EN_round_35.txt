Hydrogen isotopes have different names that remain in
common use today: $\text{}^2\text{H}$ is $\text{deuterium}[7]$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ is $\text{tritium}[8]$. The symbols D and T are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC ($\text{International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry}$) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{}^2\text{H}$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical formulas}$.[9]
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{}^1\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.
