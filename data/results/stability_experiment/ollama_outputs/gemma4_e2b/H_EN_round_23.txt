isotopes of hydrogen
From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
Hydrogen ($\text{}^{1}\text{H}$) has three naturally occurring isotopes: $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$, $\text{}^{1}\text{H}$ and $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ and $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas.[9]
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in common use today:
$\text{}^{1}\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
