Isotopes of hydrogen
Article $\text{}^{1}\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ is stable, while $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$ is tritium. The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC ($\text{International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry}$) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ and $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$ (10-21 s). Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in common use today. $\text{}^{1}\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. $\text{}^{2}\text{H}$ has half-life of $12.32$ years. $\text{}^{3}\text{H}$ has half-life of $10^{-21}$ s. During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
