Isotopes of hydrogen have different names in
common use today: $\text{2H}$, $\text{1H}$ and $\text{3H}$ isotopes
from Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia $\text{[10]}$
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in
common use today: $\text{2H}$ is $\text{deuterium}$$\text{[7]}$ and $\text{3H}$ is $\text{tritium}$$\text{[8]}$
The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; $\text{IUPAC}$ ($\text{International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry}$) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $\text{2H}$ and $\text{3H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical}$ formulas.$\text{[9]}$
Hydrogen has a $\text{half-life}$ of $12.32$ years. $\text{[5]}$
Heavier isotopes also exist; all
symbols $\text{2H}$ and $\text{3H}$ have $\text{half-life}$ of $10^{-21}$ $\text{s}$.$\text{[6]}$
During the early
study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such
names are rarely used today.
