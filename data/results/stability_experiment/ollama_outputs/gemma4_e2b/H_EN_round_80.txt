Heavier isotopes also exist; all
are synthetic and have a half-life of $10^{-21}$ s.[6]
Hydrogen is the only element
whose isotopes have different names that remain in
common use today: $^{2}\text{H}$ is deuterium[7] and $^{3}\text{H}$ is tritium.[8] The symbols D and T are
sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC
(International Union of Pure and
Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic
symbols $^{2}\text{H}$ and $^{3}\text{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of
chemical formulas.[9]
Hydrogen has $\text{neutrons}$, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early
study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such
names are rarely used today.
