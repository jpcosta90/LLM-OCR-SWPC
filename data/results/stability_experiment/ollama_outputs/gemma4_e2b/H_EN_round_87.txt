Isotopes of hydrogen
From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ²H, ¹H and ³H. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²H and ³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas.[9]
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names in common use today: ⁴H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years.[5]
