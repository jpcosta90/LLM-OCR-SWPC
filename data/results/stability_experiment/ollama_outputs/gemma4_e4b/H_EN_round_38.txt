Isotopes of hydrogen
Hydrogen ($\text{}^1\text{H}$) has three naturally occurring isotopes: $\text{}^1\text{H}$, $\text{}^2\text{H}$, and $\text{}^3\text{H}$. $\text{}^1\text{H}$ and $\text{}^2\text{H}$ are stable, while $\text{}^3\text{H}$ has a half-life of 12.32 years. $\text{}^4\text{nb}$ $\text{}^1\text{H}$ $\text{International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry}$ accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{}^2\text{H}$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of $\text{chemical formulas}$. $\text{}^9\text{1}\text{H}$, with no $\text{neutrons}$, may be called protium to disambiguate. $\text{}^{10}\text{During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.}$
