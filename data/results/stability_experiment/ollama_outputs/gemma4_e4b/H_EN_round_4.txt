Isotopes of hydrogen
Hydrogen ($\text{}^1\text{H}$) has three naturally occurring isotopes: $\text{}^1\text{H}$, $\text{}^2\text{H}$, and $\text{}^3\text{H}$. $\text{}^1\text{H}$ and $\text{}^2\text{H}$ are stable, while $\text{}^3\text{H}$ has a half-life of 12.32 years. $\text{}^4\text{nb}$ $\text{}^1\text{H}$ Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond ($10^{-21} \text{s}$). $\text{}^5\text{s}$
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: $\text{}^2\text{H}$ is deuterium[7] and $\text{}^3\text{H}$ is tritium.[8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{}^2\text{H}$ and $\text{}^3\text{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas.[9] $\text{}^1\text{H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
