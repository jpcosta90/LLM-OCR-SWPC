Isotopes of hydrogen

Article Talk

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen ($\text{^1H}$) has three naturally occurring isotopes: $\text{^1H}$, $\text{^2H}$, and $\text{^3H}$. $\text{^1H}$ and $\text{^2H}$ are stable, while $\text{^3H}$ has a half-life of 12.32 years. $\text{[4][nb 1]}$ Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond ($10^{-21}$ s). $\text{[5][6]}$

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: $\text{^2H}$ is deuterium $\text{[7]}$ and $\text{^3H}$ is tritium $\text{[8]}$. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{^2H}$ and $\text{^3H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas. $\text{[9]}$ $\text{^1H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. $\text{[10]}$ During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
