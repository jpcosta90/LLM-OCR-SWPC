Isotopes of hydrogen
Hydrogen ($\text{^1H}$) has three naturally occurring isotopes: $\text{^1H}$, $\text{^2H}$, and $\text{^3H}$. $\text{^1H}$ and $\text{^2H}$ are stable, while $\text{^3H}$ has a half-life of 12.32 years. $\text{^4He}$ also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond ($10^{-21}\text{s}$).
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: $\text{^2H}$ is deuterium[7] and $\text{^3H}$ is tritium.[8] The symbols $\text{D}$ and $\text{T}$ are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols $\text{^2H}$ and $\text{^3H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas.[9] $\text{^1H}$, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
