Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \( ^{1}^{H} \), \( ^{2}^{H} \) and \( ^{3}^{H} \). \( ^{1}^{H} \) and \( ^{2}^{H} \) are stable, while \( ^{3}^{H} \) has a half-life of 12.32 years. \( ^{1}^{H} \) and \( ^{2}^{H} \) are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zephyre(10−2¹\) s). \( ^{3}^{H} \) is heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zephyre(10−2¹) s). Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \( ^{2}^{H} \) is deuterium and \( ^{3}^{H} \) is tritium. The symbols \( ^{1}^{H} \) and \( ^{3}^{H} \) is sometimes used for deuteri um and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \( ^{2}^{H} \) and \( ^{3}^{H} \), to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.
