Isotopes of hydrogen

Isotopes of:  

\(^1\)H, \(^2\)H, and \(^3\)H. \(^1\)H and \(^2\)H are stable, while \(^3\)H has a **half-life of 12.32 years.*\[14[neq1] 1\) Heuris isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptoSECOND (10⁻²¹ s).\[5[6] \)Heuris isotopes have different names that remain in common use today : \(^2\)ₘH is deuterium; and \(^3\)ₘH is tritium.【8[8]】The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuteron and tritium; IUPAC（International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry） accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(^1H\) and \(^3H,\) to avoid confusion in alphabetical ordering of chemical formulas.[9]  
\(^1\)ₘH, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radiosotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
