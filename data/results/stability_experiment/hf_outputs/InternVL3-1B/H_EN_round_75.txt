Isotopes of hydrogen
=======================

Hydrogen (1H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^{1}\)H, \(^{2}\)H, and \(^{3}\)H. \(^{1}\)H and \(^{2}\)H are stable, while \(^{3}\)H has a half-life of 12.32 years. \(^{1}\)H and \(^{2}\)H are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosec (10\(^{-2}\) s). [5][6]
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \(^{2}\)H is deuteron and \(^{3}\)H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuteron and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(^{2}\)H and \(^{3}\)H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.
