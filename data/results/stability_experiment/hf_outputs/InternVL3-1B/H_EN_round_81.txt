Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

**1H**, \(^{1}_{2}H\), and \(^{1}_{2}H\) are naturally occurring isotopes: \(^{1}_{2}H\) and \(^{1}_{2}H\) are stable, while \(^{3}_{2}H\) has a half-life of 12.32 years.[^4][[nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10\(^{-2}\) s).[^5][[nb 6]**

Hydrogen (\textsupersize{}\textit{H}) is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \(^2H\) is deuterium and \(^3H\) is tritium.[^8] The symbols \(\mathrm{^{2H}}\) and \(\mathrm{T}^{\textit{B}}\) are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(\mathrm{^{2}H}\) and \(\mathrm{^{3}\mathrm{H}}\) to avoid confusion inalphabetic sorting of chemical formulas.[^9] Symbols \(\mathrm{^{2}H}\) and \(\mathrm{^{3}\mathrm{H}}\) with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[^10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy rad isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
