Isotopes of hydrogen

Article | Talk

Hydrogen (¹₁H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^{1}\text{H}\), \(^{2}\text{H}\), and \(^{3}\text{H}\). \(^{1}\text{H}\) and \(^{2}\text{H}》e are stable, while \(^{3}\text{H}\) has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nh] Heuris isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosec (10⁻²¹ s). [5][nh] 

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \(^{2}\text{H}\] is deuterium and \(^{3}\text{H}\] is tritium. The symbols  \(^{2}\text{H}\) and \(^{3}\text{H}\) are sometimes used for deuterium and tritymium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotop symbols \(^{2}\text{H}\) and \(^{3}\text{H}\), to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas. [9] symbols \(^{2}\text{H}\) and \(^{3}\text{H}\) may be called protium to disambiguate. [10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
