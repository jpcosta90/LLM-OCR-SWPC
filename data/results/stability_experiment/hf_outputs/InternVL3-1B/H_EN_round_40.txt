Isotopes of hydrogen

Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹H, ²H, and ³H. ¹H and ²H are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nb]1[/nb] Helium isotope ¹H². [5][br]Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²H is deuterium and ³H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deutrui and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts signaled symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(^2H\) and \(^3H\), to avoid confusion in alphabetical ordering of chemical formulas. [9] symbols \(^2H^\) and \(³H^+ \) have no neutrons, may be called propane to dyesimplify. [10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
