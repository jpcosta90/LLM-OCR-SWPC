Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (1H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ^1H, ^2H, and ^3H. 1H and 2H are stable, while \(^3H\) has a half-life of 12.32 years. \(^{1H}\) and \(^{2H}\) are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zep-tosecond (10\(^{-2}5)}[\,6] hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \(^{2H}\) is deuterium and \(^{3H}\) is tritium. The symbols \(D\) and \(T\) are sometimes used for deuteron and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotope symbols \(^{2H}\) and \(^{3H}\) to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.9 Symbols \(^{2H}\) and \(^{3H}\) represent atoms with the same atomic number.9 The recommended standard symbol \(^{3H}\) for tritium isotope symbols \(^{2H}\) and \(^{3H}\) to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.9 Symbols \(^{2H}\) and \(^{3H}\) are written with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.9 During the early study of radiation activity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
