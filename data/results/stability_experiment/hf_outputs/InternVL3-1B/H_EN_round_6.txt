Isotopes of hydrogen
From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (1H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^1\mathrm{H}\), \(^2\mathrm{H}\), and \(^\mathrm{3}\mathrm{H}\). 1\(^\mathrm{3}\mathrm{H}\) has a half-life of 12.32 years. \(^\mathrm{1}\mathrm{H}\) and \(^{\mathrm{2}}\mathrm{H}\) are stable, while \(^\mathrm{3}\mathrm{H}\) has a half-life of 12.74. less than 1 zebtose\) (10\(^{-21}\) s). [5][6]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \(^{2}\mathrm{H}$ is deuterium and \(^\mathrm{3}\mathrm{H}$ is tritium. \(^\mathrm{^\ast\,H\] and \(^\mathrm{^\ast\,+}\mathrm{^\ast\,H\) is sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(^{\upast1\,H\,\hboxand3\,H\, and^\prime\upast2\hboxand^{\prime\upast\upast\upast\,H\upast1\,{\upast2\ast}}^{\ast\ast}\) [9 symbols \(^2H\) and \(^^\ast{}^\ast\,{^\ast\ast\,{^\ast\upast\ast\,{^\ast\ast\,{^\ast\ast\ast\ast}^{\ast}}^{\ast\ast\ast\ast\,^\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast\ast}\upast2\ast\hboxacknowledged.\) [10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radionucleotides were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
