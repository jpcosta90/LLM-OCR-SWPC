Isotopes of hydrogen

Hydrogen (1H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \( ^1\mathrm{H} \), \( ^2\mathrm{H} \), and \( ^3\mathrm{H} \). \( ^1\mathrm{H} \) and \( ^2\mathrm{H} \) are stable, while \( ^3\mathrm{H} \) has a half-life of 12.32 years. 

Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zephircisecond ( \( \rm{10^{-21}\ s} \). [5][6]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \( ^{\mathrm{HI}}bacterium[7] \) and \( \mathrm{^{\mathrm{HI}}}tritium \). \( ^{\mathrm{HI}} \mathrm{ and } ^{\mathrm{HI}} \mathrm{ \mathrm{I}} \mathrm{H} \) is therefore deuteron and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \( ^{\mathrm{HI}} \mathrm{ and } ^{\mathrm{HI}} \mathrm{H} \), to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9]
