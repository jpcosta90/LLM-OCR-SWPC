Isotopes of hydrogen

**1H**, \(^1\)H, and \(2^1\)H. \(^1^1\)H Heavy isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosec (10⁻¹² s).\[\[6\] Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes that have different names that remain in common use today: \(^2^1\)H is deuteron and \(^1^1\)H is tritium. The symbols \(D\) and \(T\) are sometimes used for deuteron and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(^2^1\)H and \(^3^1^1\)H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.\[\[9\] \(^1^1\)H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. \[10\) During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
