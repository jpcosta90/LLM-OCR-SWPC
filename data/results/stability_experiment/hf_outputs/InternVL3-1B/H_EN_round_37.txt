Hydrogen (\textsupersize 1H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^\text{1H}\), \(^\text{2H}\) and \(^\text{3H}\). \(^\text{1H}\) and \(^\text{2H}\) are stable, while \(^\text{3H}\) has a \textquote from \textcite number 11] \[4^\text{nb}\, \text{1} ^\text{H}\) \[^\text{half-f}i\text{e} \text{of}(1 \text{ zeptosec} \, (\text{10}^{-21}\text{ s})]).^\text{half}{\text-}life \text{ of less than 1 \text{ zeptosec} \text{ (10}^-21\text{ s})}.\] \[^\text{half-f}i\text{e} \text{ of}(1 \text{ zeptosec} \text{ (10}^-21\text{ s})}\]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \(^\text{1H}\) is deuterium and \(^\text{3H}\) is tritium. The symbols \(
\pmb{^\text{D}}\) and \(\pmb{^\pmb{T}}\) are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applicable Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(\pmb{^\text{D}}\) and \(\pmb{^\text{T}}\) to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.
