Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

1\.H and 2\.H are naturally occurring isotopes and all hydrogen isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10\_-2\ s).\[5[/6] Heavier isotopes also exist; all hydrogen isotopes have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10\_-2\ s).

Hydrogen (\emph{H}) has three naturally occurring isotopes: 1\,\text{H}, 2\,\text{H}, and 3\,\text{H}. 1\,\text{H} and 2\,\text{H} are stable, while 3\,\text{H} has a half-life of 12.32 years.\[4\,\text{(nb}1\right) Heuristic isotope notation: .\]He isotope \emph{o\,-\,i-\,d}.d and 3\,\text{H} is tritium.\[8\]The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuteron and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ^2\textupilde p and ^3\textupmilde H,...\]t to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas[.9\]
