Isotopes of hydrogen

**Talk**

**Hydrogen** has three naturally occurring isotope:
1H, \(^1\)H, and \(3^1\)H. \(1H\) and \(2^1\)H are stable, while \(3^1\)H has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosec (10\(^{-2}\) s). [5][6]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today:
\(^2H\) is deuterium and \(^3H\) is tritium. The symbols \(D\) and \(T\) are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(^2H\) and \(^3H\) to avoid confusion in alphabetically of chemical formulas.[9] symbols \(2^H\) and \(3^H\), to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[10]

During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
