Modern science is typically divided into two or three — or three — major branches: the natural sciences, which study the physical world, and the social sciences, which study individuals and societies.[3]While referred to as the formal sciences, the study of logic, mathematics, computer science are typically regarded as separate because they rely on deductive reasoning instead of the scientific methodology. Nevertheless, applied sciences are disciplines that use scientific knowledge for practical purposes, such as engineering and medicine.[4][5][6][7][8][9] Meanwhile, applied sciences are typically regarded as separate to two or three, major — or three — major branches: informal sciences, the study of logic, mathematics, computer science, and theoretical computer science are typically regarded as separate because they rely on deductive reasoning instead of the scientific method as their main methodology.[6][7][8][9] Meanwhile, applied sciences are disciplines that use scientific knowledge for practical purposes, such as engineering and medicine.[4][5][6][7][8][9] The history of science spans the majority of the historical record, with the earliest identifiable precessors to modern science dating to the Bronze Age in Egypt and Mesopotamia (c. 3300–1200 BCE). Their contributions to mathematics, astronomy, and medicine entered and shaped the Greek natural philosophy of classical antiquity and later medieval scholarship, whereby formal attempts were made to provide explanations of events in the physical world based on natural causes; further advances, including the introduction of the Hindu-Arabic numeral system, were made during the Golden Age of India and Islamic Golden Age.[12]:12[13][14][15][16][17][18][19][20].
