Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²H, ²H, and ³³H. ¹H and ²H are stable, while ³³H has a half-life of 12.32 years.[4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10²⁻²¹ s).[5][6] Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²H² is deuterium [7] and ³³HH is tritium [8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²²H ³³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9][10] ¹₃H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to avoid confusion.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioactive isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
