Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes:¹²H, and³³H.¹H and and²₂H are stable, while³³H has a half-life of 12.32 years.[4][4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (¹0²ⁱ s).[5][6]Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common usage today:²²H is deuterium and³³H is tritium.[7][8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols²²H and³³H,[9] to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[6][9]¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
