Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²H, ²H, and ³³H. ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years.[4][nb 1] Heavy isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10−21 s).[5][6] 

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ^2⁸H is deuterium and ^3⁸H is tritium.[7][8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ^2H and ^3H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9][10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioactive isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.

1H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioactive isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
