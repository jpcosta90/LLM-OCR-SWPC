Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹H and ²H, and ³²H, ¹³H have a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zep-to-seconds (10⁻²¹ s). [5][6] Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²²H is deuterium[7] and ³²H is tritium.[8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²²H and ³²H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9]¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. [10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioactive isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
