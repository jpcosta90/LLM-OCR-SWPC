Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹H and ²H, and ³H. ¹H and ²H are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavy isotope also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10⁻²¹ s).[4][4][nb 1] Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²H is deuterium and ³H is tritium.[7][8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols D and T to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9]
¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
