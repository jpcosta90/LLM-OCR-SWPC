Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹H, ²H, and ³H.¹H and ²H are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavy isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10⁻²¹ s).  

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²H is deuterium and ³H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²H and ³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioactive isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
