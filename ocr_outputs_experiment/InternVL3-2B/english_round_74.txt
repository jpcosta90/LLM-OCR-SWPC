Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes:¹²²H, and ³³H.¹H and ²²²H are stable, while ³³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][[nb 1]] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10⁻²¹ s).[5[[nb 6]] Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²²²H is deuterium and ³³H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²²²H and ³³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioactive isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
