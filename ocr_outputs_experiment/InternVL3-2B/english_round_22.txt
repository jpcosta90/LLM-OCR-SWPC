Hydrogen (${ }_{1}^{1} \mathrm{H}$) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ${ }_{1}^{2} \mathrm{H}$, and ${ }_{1}^{3} \mathrm{H}$. ${ }_{1}^{1} \mathrm{H}$ and ${ }_{2}^{2} \mathrm{H}$ are stable, while ${ }_{3}^{3} \mathrm{H}$ has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond $(10^{-21} \mathrm{~s~})$.  Hydrogen is the only [bold italic]element whose isotopes have different names that remain in [bold italic]common current use today.[1]${ }_{2}^{H}$ is deuterium[7], and ${ }_{3}^{H}$ is tritium.[8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ${ }_{2}^{2} \mathrm{H}$ and ${ }_{3}^{3} \mathrm{H}$, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.
