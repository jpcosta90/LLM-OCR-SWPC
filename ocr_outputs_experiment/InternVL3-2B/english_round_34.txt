Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹⁶H, and ³¹H. ¹H and ²H and ²¹H are stable, while ³¹H has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavy isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10⁻²¹ s).  
Hydrogen is the only [color]element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common [color]use today: ²¹H is [color]deuterium, [color]³¹H is [color]tritium.] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²¹H and ³¹H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas. ¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to to disambiguate. During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
