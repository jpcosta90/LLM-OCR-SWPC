Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (¹ₗ) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²H, and ³H. ¹H and ²H are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zentosecond (1₀₋₁₂₂⁻¹⁷⁹⁶⁹⁶⁶⁶⁶⁶⁶⁶⁶⁶⁶⁶⁶ s).[5][6] Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²⁶H is deuterium[7] and ³⁶H is tritium.[8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²⁶H and ³⁶H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9]¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[¹⁦] During the early study of radioactiveity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
