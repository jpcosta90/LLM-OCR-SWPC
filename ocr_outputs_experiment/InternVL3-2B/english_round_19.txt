Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (¹₁H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹₁H, ²₁H, and ³₁H.¹₁H and ²₁H are stable, while ³₁H has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavy isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10⁻²¹ s).[4][5][6] Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²₁H is deuterium[7] and ³₁H is tritium.[8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAP (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²₁H and ³₁H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9]
