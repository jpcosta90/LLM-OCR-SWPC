Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹² ²H, and ³ ³H. ¹H and ²H are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavy isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zentosecond (10⁻²¹ s).

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ² ²H is deuterium and ³ H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ² ²H and ³ H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.¹ H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.¹ H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
