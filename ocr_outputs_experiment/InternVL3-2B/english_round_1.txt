Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ² H is deuterium[7] and ³ H is tritium[8]. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ² H and ³ H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9][10] ¹ H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[11] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioactive isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
