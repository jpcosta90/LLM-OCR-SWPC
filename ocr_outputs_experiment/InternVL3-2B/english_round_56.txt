Hydrogen (¹₅ H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²₆H, and ³₂H. ¹²₆H and ¹²₆H are stable, while ³H³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10²⁻¹ s). Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²₆H is deuterium and ³H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²HH and ^3H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.¹₈¹¹ H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.¹⁹ During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
