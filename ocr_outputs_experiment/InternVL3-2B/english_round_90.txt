Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²²H, and ³³H. ¹H and ²²H are stable, while ³³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zentoseconds (10⁻²¹ s).¹H, without neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.

The element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²²H is deuterium and ³³H is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²²H and ³³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to avoid confusion.
