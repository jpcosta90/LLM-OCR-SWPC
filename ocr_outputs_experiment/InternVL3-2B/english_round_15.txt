Hydrogen (¹ₗ) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²ₖH, and ³ᵞH. ¹ₙ and ²ₖH are stable, while ³ₙH has a half-life of 12.32 years. Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zentoseconds (10-21 s).[5][6]   Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different common names that remain in  common use today: ²ₖH is deuterium[7] and ³ₙH is tritium.[8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ²ₖH and ³ₙH, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9]¹ₙ₁, with no neutrons,  may be called protium to disambiguate.[10]   During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
