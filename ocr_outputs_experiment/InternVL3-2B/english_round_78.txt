Isotopes of hydrogen

Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²H, and ¹²¹H, and ³H. ¹H and ²H are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. ³¹H and ²²¹H are also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10⁻²¹ s).[ref1][ref2]  
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: ²²H is deuterium[ref7] and ³³H is tritium.[ref8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry)[ref9] accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols ${2^2}$H and ${3^3}$H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas. ¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.[ref10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names but such names are rarely used today.
