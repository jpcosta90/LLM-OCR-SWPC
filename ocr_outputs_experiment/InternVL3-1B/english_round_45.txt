Isotopes of hydrogen
---

Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \( ^{1}H,\ ^{2}H,\) and \( ^{3}H.\) \( ^{1}H\) and \( ^{2}H\) are stable, while \( ^{{3}}H\) has a half-life of 12.32 years.[4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zetasecond (¹₀⁻²¹ s). Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \( ^{2}H\) is deuterium\( and \( ^{3}H\) is tritium. The symbols \( D\) and \( T \) are sometimes used for deutium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \( ^{2}H\) and \( ^{3}H,\) to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas. \( ^{1}H,\) with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.
