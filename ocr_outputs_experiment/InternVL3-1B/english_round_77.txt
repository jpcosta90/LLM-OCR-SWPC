Hydrogen (1H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \( ^1H\), and \( ^3H\). \( ^1H\) and \( ^2H\) are stable, while \( ^3H\) has a \(\pmb{fsub}life of 12.32 years. [4][nb1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zetacept (10{-}21 s).\) .[5][6]
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \( ^2H\) is deuteron\( and \( ^3H\) is tritium. The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deutium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applicable Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \( ^2H\) and \( ^3H\), to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.
