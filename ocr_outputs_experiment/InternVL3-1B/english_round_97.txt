Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (1H), has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^1_2H^+\) and \(^\mathbf{1}^2H_{\mathbf{3}}^{\mathbf{3}}\) \([4][nh_{\mathbf{l}}]^{1}\) Hydroisotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of 12.32 years. \(^1\mathbf{10}^{-21}s\) \(\mathbf{[5][h]}\) Tertiobond (10\textbf{\-}^{-21}s). [5][s] IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but suggests the standard isotopic symbols \(^2H\) and \(\mathbf{^3}H\) to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas. \(\mathbf{{}^\mathbf{\text{H}}^\mathbf{\text{2}}}\) \(\mathbf{^{\mathbf{\text{3}}H}}_{\mathbf{\text{3}}\mathbf{H}}\) with no \(\mathbf{neutrons}$, may be called \(\mathbf{pt}u\mathbf{r}\) to disarmage. \(\mathbf{1H}\) with \(\mathbf{neutrons}\), may be called \(\mathbf{pt}p\mathbf{r}\) to disambiguate. During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such \(\mathbf{names}\) are rarely used today.
