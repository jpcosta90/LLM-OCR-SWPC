Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

---

Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes:¹H and ²H, and ³H. ¹H and ²H are stable, while ³H has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptoseond (10⁻² s). [5][6]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today:²H is deuterium[7] and³H is tritium.[8] The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deutium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols²H and³H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas. [9] symbols²H and [3H] are recommended at [10]. ¹H, with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. [10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radioisotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
