Isotopes of hydrogen
From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (1H), has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^1H,^2H, \) and \(^{3H}.\) \(^{1}H\) and \(^{2}H\) are stable, while \(^{3}H\) has a half-life of 12.32 years.[4]Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10\(^{-2}s\).). [5][6] 
Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \(^2H\) is deuteron and \(^{3H}\) is tritium. The symbols \(D\) and \(T\) are sometimes used for deutrium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(^{2}H\) and \(^{3}H,\) to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas.
