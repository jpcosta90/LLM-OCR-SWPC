Isotopes of hydrogen

From Free Encyclopedia

Hydrogen (1H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^1\)H, and \(^3\)H. \(^\text{3}^i\)H has a half-life of 12.32 years.[4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zep-tosec (10\(^{-2}\) s).](5][br][br]The symbols D and T are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(^1\)H and \(^3\)H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9] symbols \(^2\)H and \(^3\)H, to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.[9] \(^1\)C,\(^2\)H,\(^3\)C,\(^2\)C,\(^3\)C,[6] protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radiotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
