Isotopes of hydrogen

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Hydrogen (¹H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹H, and ³H. \(1^\mathrm{H}\) and \(2^\mathrm{H}\) are stable, while \(3^\mathrm{H}\) has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nb ¹] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zetapectometric (10⁻²¹ s). [5][6]

Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \(2^\mathrm{H}\) is deleterium \(and\) \(3^\mathrm{H}\) is tritium. [8] The symbols \(D\) and \(T\) are sometimes used for deuterymium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(2^\mathrm{H}\) and \(3^\mathrm{H}\) to avoid confusion in alphabetic sorting of chemical formulas. [9] symbols \(2^\mathrm{H}\) and \[3^\mathrm{H}\) are \(^{1^\mathrm{H}}\) with no neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate. \[^\mathrm{1^\mathrm{H}}\] with no neutrons, may be called proton to disambige. [10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
