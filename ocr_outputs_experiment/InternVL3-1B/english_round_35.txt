### Isotopes of hydrogen

Hydrogen (¹H), has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^{1}\!,\) and \(^{3}\!\mathrm{H}\). \(^{1}\mathrm{H}\) and \(^{2}\mathrm{H}\) are stable, while \(^{3}\mathrm{H}\) has a half-life of 12.32 years.**\[4]^{1}\mathrm{nb}^{1}^{1}^{1}\)** Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosec (10\(^{-21}\) s).**[5][6]** Hydrogen is the only element whose isotopes have different names that remain in common use today: \(^{2}\!,\) deuteron and \(^{3}\!\mathrm{H}\) is triumtium.**\[8\) The symbols \(D\) and \(T\) are sometimes used for deutrium and tritium; **IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry)** accepts said symbols, but depends on the standard isotopic symbols \(^{1}\!\mathrm{H}\) and \(^{3}\!\mathrm{H}\), to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas.\[9\] symbols \(^{1}\!\mathrm{H}\), without neutrons, may be called protium to disambiguate.**10** During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy isotopes were given names, but such names are rarely used today.
