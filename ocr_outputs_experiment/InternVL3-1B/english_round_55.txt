Hydrogen (₁H) has three naturally occurring isotopes: \(^{1}\mathrm{H}\), and \(^{3}\mathrm{H}\). \(^{1}\mathrm{H}\) and \(^{2}\mathrm{H}\) are stable, while \(^{3}\mathrm{H}\) has a half-life of 12.32 years. [4][nb 1] Heavier isotopes also exist; all are synthetic and have a half-life of less than 1 zeptosecond (10⁻²¹ s). [5][6] The symbols \(D\) and \(T\) are sometimes used for deutium and tritium; IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) accepts said symbols, but recommends the standard isotopic symbols \(^{2}\mathrm{H}$ and \(^{3}\mathrm{H}\), to avoid confusion in alphabetical sorting of chemical formulas [9] symbols, but [10] called protium to disambiguate.[10] During the early study of radioactivity, some other heavy radios are given names, but such names are rarely used today.
